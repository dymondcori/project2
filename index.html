  • In the late 1850’s, Edouard-Leon Scott de Martinville, successfully invented an audio recording device called the phonautograph. This device, however, could not playback the audio.

    It wasn’t until Thomas Edison invented the phonograph in 1877 that music and sound could be both recorded and played back. While the technology was revolutionary, the phonograph had terrible sound quality and only allowed users to play back the audio one time.

    Source 1, Source 2

  • Ten years later the gramophone was invented. The machine often functioned through a hand crank and played disks that looked like LP records. As the Gramaphone was cranked, a needle would read the etchings on the record as it rotated. This transmitted the sounds through the speaker. By 1900, the records could be massed produced.

  • Sony released a personal and portable stereo tape player, the Walkman, revolutionizing the way people listened to music. People could now listen to music wherever they went through headphones.

    In 1983, cassette’s outsold vinyl records for the first time ever.

    The technology continued to grow and new features were added, such as rechargeable batteries, AM/FM radio, and audio reverse.

  • The first commercial and standardized compact disk was a significant start of the digital age. Where music had been read mechanically in the past, it now used electric signals to read encoded data. This was a huge step for audio technology. CDs became the prominent method of music playing for the next decade, and, although they are much less popular, are still used today.

  • The invention of MP3 came with the rise of computers and the Internet. Karlheinz Brandenburg began researching and experimenting with transmitting music over phone lines in 1982. After years of experimenting, his efforts were successful.

    In 1995 an English entrepreneur asked Brandenburg, “Do you know that this will destroy the music industry?” and he was right.

  • Today, most people stream their music through their smartphones, laptops, and tablets. Anyone can record sounds and songs on their various devices or buy and store their music on iTunes.
